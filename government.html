Role in Climate Change

As time passes, First Nations are asserting themselves, taking a stronger role in climate planning and environmental governance. A strategy states that Indigenous peoples must be recognized as inherent Title and Rights holders, with the authority to govern and manage their traditional territories. Meaning, they become governments of their own with responsibilities of one ideally cooperating with the province to combat the effect of climate change.


Legal Foundations

DRIPA, Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act, passed by the BC government in 2019 requires all provincial laws be in accordance with the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. The UNDRIP is a powerful baseline aimed at every single country in the world, defining the bare minimum of what countries should be doing related to their Indigenous people. While not legally binding, it still serves a point in giving a base on which countries can build up on.


Emergency Response & Managment

To most communities, the largest risks they face is the risk of a wildfire, on which the BC government decided to partner with First Nations to provide training on how to act during these devastating events.


Case Study: The Great Bear Rainforest

The Great Bear Rainforest agreement is an extremely important Indigenous-led protection agreements up to date. Negotiated between Coastal First Nations and the BC government, it now protects 6.4 million hectares of old-growth trees. The protection of this is crucial, as it is one big sink for carbon emissions as well as protecting old-growth trees, which are culturally significant in some communities/nations.
