How does climate change impact First Nations in B.C.?

What is climate change?

Climate change is the shift in global temperatures along with changes in the behaviour of weather mainly caused by humans. Climate changes occur when greenhouse gasses (Carbon dioxide or Methane) are released in mass amounts. Greenhouse gasses act as a blanket, keeping the heat the sun radiates into the Earth and trapping it in. This raises global temperatures to unnatural numbers, putting at risk life on Earth. The effects of climate change can manifest in many ways, like extremely hot summers and extremely cold winters, as devastating storms that last for weeks or even no storms at all. Which in some regions could lead to droughts.

What dangers does it pose?

The threat of climate change requires immediate action, as its long-term effects can be irreversible or last for hundreds of years. Higher global temperatures mean glaciers begin to melt, releasing tons of extra water into the ocean. This will cause the sea levels to rise and permanently flood some countries. The weather changes are equally destructive, as storms could appear and take weeks before they leave, featuring wind speeds higher than what it normally should be. Droughts of the same scale can happen, as regions could begin to dry up due to rain cycles being disrupted.

How does this affect indigenous communities?

Effect on indigenous communities

Indigenous communities are already experiencing the effects of climate change. The rise of global temperatures have led to worsening wildfire seasons, causing destruction of their lands and hunting ground. In other areas, floods have become even more common, causing forced relocation of the entire community. These changes are not contained to only physical things, the effects are affecting how ecosystems exist. Wildlife is also being severely affected. There have been reports on declining moose population in certain areas along with berries and other forage struggling to ripen as the seasonal changes become less and less reliable.
