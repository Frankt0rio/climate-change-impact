How does Climate Change Affect Salmon?

In many indigenous communities, salmon, herring, shellfish and similar sea animals are crucial to their culture and diet. There are stories shaped about fish and other communities treat salmon with the utmost respect. Climate change is shrinking and displacing the population of these fish, making catching them harder. Scientists believe/estimate that BC First Nations fisheries catches could drop by a staggering 49% when we get to 2050.

What Does This Mean For First Nations?

Ecosystem Impact

Land-based food supplies are affected too. Berries and plants used for medicinal purposes are ripening at times they shouldn't be, and as Gitxsan harvesters noted, there was visible heat stress on some of their forage after a heatwave. Moose and other wildlife have been rarer to encounter and force hunters to travel much farther for a successful hunt. Indigenous elders point out that everything is connected, if something is affected it causes the whole system to shift.

Cultural Impact

For many First Nations, salmon among other marine species hold huge cultural value, they are woven into stories, spiritual beliefs and ceremonies. There are big events solely focused on harvesting them and making up a big part of their identities.

Economic Impact

Other than being culturally heavy, they provide a portion of money for many families and First Nations generated from the harvesting of marine species. A drop so big in fish catches could mean $6.7 to $12 million CAD being lost in possible revenue annually.

Food Security

Communities living in remote locations that rely on catching their own food will have a big punch to the gut. Not only will it be harder to catch food, but the income will be reduced. Imported foods are generally way more expensive in indigenous communities and not as nutritious as the natural wildlife in the area.

Critical Thinking

What do we think?

When talking about food, we all think of what we want to eat, our favourite dish and similar things. But we fail to realize that for others, this topic might be sensitive. First Nations are on the early stages of losing a big part of their cultures, fishing and hunting. Due to climate change, their self-sufficiency is in question and we believe there is not a lot being done about this.
