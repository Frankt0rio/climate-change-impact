Wildfires in British Columbia

Record Breaking Wildfires

In recent years, BC forests have been under siege from worsening wildfires. In 2023, the BC was put under what is described as the worst wildfire season. With 2.84M hectares of land burned, it had a total 2200 fires with 400 active at the same time. This event included 60 fires of Note (Fires that posed a serious threat to public safety). The wildfire disaster forced the evacuation of approximately 48,000 people, many of which were indigenous.

Unfortunate events

Destruction Of Lytton

One iconic and devastating examples of what a wildfire can cause, is the destruction of Lytton. Lytton was a village that was destroyed by a fire in one of the most extreme heatwaves in recorded history. This heatwave elevated temperatures upwards of 50 degrees celsius and killed around six-hundred people.

Effect on First Nations & Indigenous Communities

Impact on First Nations

BC has a lot of First Nations in it, many of which are located outside of cities and urban areas. These communities are located in rural and forested areas which makes them especially threatened by the risk of a wildfire. The flames carried out by these wildfires can destroy sacred and cultural sites along with destroying sacred hunting ground. Wildfire smoke can and will contribute to long-term respiratory issues and complicate the health of those who already have them.

Psychological Toll

The psychological toll to this is terrible, imagine if you had a faulty heater that worsens over time and could eventually put your house on fire, with no way of removing it. That is how indigenous communities feel, living under the constant threat of being displaced and losing their sacred lands to a devastating fire that can only increase in chance of occurring. Hunting, fishing and other activities are being affected by the increasing fires these recent years.

Cultural Burning

As a way to adapt to the changing climate and wildfires landscape, First Nations have begun practicing cultural burning at a greater scale. Cultural burning is a controlled fire, aimed at the underbrush of forests that can act as a firestarter if left unchecked. In 2024, First Nations carried out 48 cultural burns, treating a total of 3,412 hectares.

Critical Thinking

What do we think?

We can see that the environment is clearly changing at a fast pace. Maybe not fast enough for us all of us to care, but it's the indigenous communities that are on the frontlines, facing all the early symptoms of the changing environment. The increased frequency of wildfires is utterly devastating everyone, especially first nations. We believe that indigenous voices should be heard even louder and even greater punishments for people that disrespect the First Nations when there are campfire bans.
